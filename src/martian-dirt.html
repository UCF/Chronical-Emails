Chances are when humans return to the moon or travel to Mars, University of Central Florida researchers will have a hand in pioneering the ways we grow food or build shelter. That’s because UCF is designing and selling simulated alien dirt to engineers and scientists around the world to unlock some of the biggest mysteries in space exploration and off-world settlement.
Since UCF’s ingenious research enterprise into experimental extraterrestrial soil opened in 2018, the Exolith Lab has teamed with NASA, research universities and private space companies using its dirt to test equipment being developed for Mars, moon and asteroid missions. Scientists building everything from solar panels to mining rovers first experiment with UCF’s simulants before launching them into space.
UCF’s simulants were created based on data from meteorites and the Curiosity rover, which landed on Mars in 2012 and contains other UCF-developed technology. Some of its commercial and educational uses are:
Pioneering the development of lunar concrete to reduce the cost of construction on the moon
Testing the sample collector for NASA’s 2024 mission to the Martian moon Phobos
Transforming terrestrial sand and lunar regolith into solar panels to generate power in space
Delivering more than 2 tons of simulant to student teams around the world for NASA’s Plant the Moon and Plant Mars growing food challenges
