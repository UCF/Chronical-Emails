The University of Central Florida is becoming America’s Space University: a space research leader and talent linchpin supporting NASA, SpaceX, Lockheed Martin and others in the aerospace industry. Today, our faculty, alumni, and students are key contributors to the Artemis missions, helping return humankind to the moon in 2025.
Artemis I – scheduled to launch this year – will be remembered as the foundation for intergalactic exploration, with more than 30 UCF alumni at NASA having played crucial roles in expanding the horizon for humankind. Through involvement in Artemis, we embrace our belief in unleashing the potential of people and ideas and inspire a new generation to enrich the human experience.
Our world-renowned faculty are inventors and leaders who are shaping the next generation of space industry talent. Their research programs, conducted with NASA, include projects like Artemis I, as well as over a dozen lunar missions. Through innovative research and groundbreaking discoveries, our experts in engineering, physics, and planetary sciences unlock the possibilities of the future with forward-thinking solutions.
Exploring the Unknown
Only 5% of the moon has been explored— for now. UCF planetary scientists Kerri Donaldson Hanna and Adrienne Dove seek to map those areas with their $35 million NASA mission to land a spacecraft on the mysterious Gruithuisen Domes. By studying the domes’ chemical composition, researchers can discover resources and insights to enable long-term exploration of the moon.
Preserving Spacecraft Integrity
Moon landings require technological excellence for spacecraft to descend upon an uneven surface of rocks and craters. Through a $500,000 Artemis grant, UCF Professor and principal investigator Seetha Raghavan is leading the development of 3D printed sensors and coatings to advance the safety of future lunar landings far beyond what was possible with the Apollo program.
Revolutionizing Lunar Landings
Rockets can sandblast lunar dust at more than 10,000 mph when they take off or land, which can severely damage equipment and historic landing sites. UCF planetary scientists Phil Metzger and Dhaka Sapkota are developing cutting-edge technology that could be used to construct cost-effective landing pads in space. Metzger, who was recently featured in USA Today, is helping lead this NASA-funded research project.
Solving Cosmic Problems
Dust particles can be hazardous for astronauts, their spacecraft and equipment. Data by UCF Professor Joshua Colwell, director of the Center of Microgravity Research, adds to the existing body of leading UCF space dust research by specifying how to reduce its impact on equipment and astronauts.
As NASA prepares to send humans back to the moon, UCF alumni and faculty will continue to support their efforts and drive our exploration deeper into space for the future of humankind.
